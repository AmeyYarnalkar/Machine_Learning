When you have many input features $x_1, x_2, x_3, \dots , x_n$ and an output label $y$, Bayes theorem becomes the foundation of the **Naive Bayes** model.

The key assumption:
All features $x_1, x_2, ..., x_n$ are **conditionally independent given $y$**.

---

## Step 1 — Start with Bayes theorem

For features $X=(x_1,x_2,...,x_n)$:

$$
P(y|x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n) = \frac{P(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n|y)P(y)}{P(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n)}
$$
Meaning:

Probability of class **y** given all feature values.

---

## Step 2 — Apply the independence assumption

Because features are **independent given (y)**:

$$
P(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n|y)
=

P(x_1|y)P(x_2|y)\dots P(x_n|y)
$$

So Bayes becomes:

$$
P(y|x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n)
=

\frac{P(y)\prod_{i=1}^{n} P(x_i|y)}
{P(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n)}
$$

---

## Step 3 — What ML models actually use

The denominator is the **same for all classes**, so classifiers ignore it.

So prediction becomes:

$$
P(y|x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n)
\propto
P(y)\prod_{i=1}^{n} P(x_i|y)
$$

The model picks the class with the **largest value**.

---

## Example (Spam Detection)

Features:

* $x_1$ = word "free"
* $x_2$ = word "offer"
* $x_3$ = word "money"

Class:

* $y$ = spam or not spam

Naive Bayes computes:

$$
P(\text{spam}) \times
P(x_1|\text{spam}) \times
P(x_2|\text{spam}) \times
P(x_3|\text{spam})
$$

and compares it with

$$
P(\text{not spam}) \times
P(x_1|\text{not spam}) \times
P(x_2|\text{not spam}) \times
P(x_3|\text{not spam})
$$

Whichever is larger → predicted class.
