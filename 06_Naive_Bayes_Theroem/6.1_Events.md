In probability, **independent** and **dependent** events describe whether one event affects the probability of another. Let’s make it very concrete.

---

## 1. Independent Events

Independent Events

Two events are **independent** if the occurrence of one **does not change the probability** of the other.

### Mathematical Rule

For independent events:

$$
P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)
$$

---

### Example 1: Coin and Dice

Event A → Getting **Head** when tossing a coin
Event B → Getting **4** when rolling a die

These are independent because **coin toss does not affect the die result**.

Probability:

$$
P(A) = \frac{1}{2}
$$

$$
P(B) = \frac{1}{6}
$$

$$
P(A \cap B) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{12}
$$

So the probability of **Head AND 4** is **1/12**.

---

### Example 2: Two Coin Tosses

Event A → First toss is **Head**
Event B → Second toss is **Head**

Again independent.

$$
P(A) = \frac{1}{2},\quad P(B) = \frac{1}{2}
$$

$$
P(A \cap B) = \frac{1}{4}
$$

---

## 2. Dependent Events

Dependent Events

Events are **dependent** if the occurrence of one event **changes the probability** of the other.

### Mathematical Rule

$$
P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B|A)
$$

Where
$P(B|A)$ = probability of B **given A already happened**

---

### Example: Cards Without Replacement

A deck has **52 cards**.

Event A → First card is **Ace**

$$
P(A) = \frac{4}{52}
$$

Event B → Second card is **Ace**

But after removing one Ace, only **3 Aces remain out of 51 cards**.

$$
P(B|A) = \frac{3}{51}
$$

So

$$
P(A \cap B) =
\frac{4}{52} \times \frac{3}{51}
$$

This is **dependent** because the first draw affects the second.

---

# Easy Way to Remember

| Situation                             | Type        |
| ------------------------------------- | ----------- |
| Coin toss + dice roll                 | Independent |
| Two coin tosses                       | Independent |
| Picking cards **with replacement**    | Independent |
| Picking cards **without replacement** | Dependent   |
