

# Step 3  

## Updating Sample Weights

After training the first decision stump and calculating its **model weight (α)**, the next step is to **update the weights of the training samples**.

The goal of this step is simple:

**Make the next weak learner focus more on the difficult samples.**

---

## Idea Behind Weight Update

After prediction, the samples fall into two groups:

1. **Correctly classified samples**
2. **Misclassified samples**

AdaBoost updates the weights differently for these two groups.

* **Misclassified samples → weight increases**
* **Correctly classified samples → weight decreases**

This ensures that the **next decision stump pays more attention to the samples that were previously misclassified**.

---

## Weight Update Rule

Let:

* $w_i$ = current weight of sample (i)
* $\alpha$ = performance (model weight) of the stump

Then the weights are updated as follows.

---

### For Misclassified Samples

$$
w_i = w_i \times e^{\alpha}
$$

The weight increases because $e^{\alpha} > 1$.

This makes these samples **more likely to influence the next model**.

---

### For Correctly Classified Samples

$$
w_i = w_i \times e^{-\alpha}
$$

The weight decreases because $e^{-\alpha} < 1$.

These samples become **less important in the next iteration**.

---

## Important Step — Weight Normalization

After updating weights, they must be **normalized** so that the total weight remains **1**.

$$
w_i = \frac{w_i}{\sum w_i}
$$

This ensures:

$$
\sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i = 1
$$

Normalization keeps the weights **stable and interpretable as probabilities**.

---

## Why This Works

This mechanism forces the algorithm to **adapt to mistakes**.

Each new weak learner is trained on a dataset where:

* hard samples have **high importance**
* easy samples have **low importance**

This is why the algorithm is called:

AdaBoost

**Adaptive Boosting**, because it **adapts to previous errors**.
