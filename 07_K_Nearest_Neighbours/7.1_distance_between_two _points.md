

## 1. Manhattan Distance


### Purpose

Measures distance by moving **only along axes (horizontal and vertical)**.
It represents how far two points are if you move like **city blocks**.

Used in:

* **K-Nearest Neighbors**
* clustering
* grid-based path problems

It is also called **L1 distance**.

---

## Formula (n-dimensional)

$$
D = \sum_{i=1}^{n} |x_i - y_i|
$$

Where:

* $x_i$ = coordinate of point A
* $y_i$ = coordinate of point B
* $n$ = number of dimensions

---

## 2D Example

Points:

A = (2,3)
B = (5,7)

Distance:

$$
|2-5| + |3-7|
$$

$$
= 3 + 4
$$

$$
= 7
$$

So Manhattan distance = **7**.

---

## Multi-Dimensional Example

Points:

A = (1,2,3,4)
B = (3,5,1,7)

$$
|1-3| + |2-5| + |3-1| + |4-7|
$$

$$
= 2 + 3 + 2 + 3
$$

$$
= 10
$$

# 2. Euclidean Distance


### Purpose

Measures the **straight-line distance between two points**.

This is the **actual geometric distance**.

Used in:

* **KNN**
* clustering
* recommendation systems
* similarity calculations

Also called **L2 distance**.

---

## Formula (n-dimensional)

$$
D =
\sqrt{
\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - y_i)^2
}
$$

---

## 2D Example

Points:

A = (2,3)
B = (5,7)

Distance:

$$
\sqrt{(2-5)^2 + (3-7)^2}
$$

$$
\sqrt{9 + 16}
$$

$$
\sqrt{25}
$$

$$
= 5
$$

So Euclidean distance = **5**.

---

## Multi-Dimensional Example

Points:

A = (1,2,3)
B = (4,6,5)

$$
\sqrt{(1-4)^2 + (2-6)^2 + (3-5)^2}
$$

$$
\sqrt{9 + 16 + 4}
$$

$$
\sqrt{29}
$$

---

## Key Difference

| Metric    | Movement style | Formula idea                       |
| --------- | -------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Manhattan | grid movement  | sum of absolute differences        |
| Euclidean | straight line  | square root of squared differences |

---

## Intuition

Imagine two locations in a city:

Manhattan distance → drive through streets \
Euclidean distance → fly directly by drone
