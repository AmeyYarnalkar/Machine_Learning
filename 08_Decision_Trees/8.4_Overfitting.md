## Overfitting in Decision Trees

Even if a decision tree finds the **best possible splits using Information Gain or Gini**, it can still easily **overfit the training data**.

Overfitting means the model **learns the training data too specifically**, including patterns that **do not generalize to new data**.

Decision trees are especially prone to overfitting because they **keep splitting until the nodes become very pure**.

---

## Why Decision Trees Overfit

### 1. Multicollinearity is Not Handled Well

Multicollinearity occurs when **multiple features are highly correlated with each other**.

Example:

```text
House Size
Number of Rooms
Living Area
```

These features often carry **similar information**.

A decision tree may:

* split on one correlated feature
* then split again on another correlated feature

This leads to **redundant splits**, making the tree unnecessarily complex.

Because of this, the model starts **memorizing the training data instead of learning general patterns**.

---

### 2. Noise in the Data

Real-world datasets usually contain **noise**, such as:

* incorrect labels
* measurement errors
* random fluctuations

Decision trees try to **perfectly separate the data**, so they may create splits that only explain **random noise**.

Example:

```text
If Age > 32 AND Income > 51,200 → Class A
```

This rule might only fit **a few noisy training samples** and may not apply to new data.

As a result, the model becomes **too specific**.

---

### 3. Deep Trees Memorize the Training Data

Decision trees can grow **very deep**, creating many nodes.

When this happens:

* each leaf node may contain **very few samples**
* the model effectively **memorizes the dataset**

This causes **excellent training accuracy but poor test accuracy**.

---

### 4. Greedy Splitting Strategy

Decision trees use a **greedy approach**, meaning:

* at each step, they choose the **locally best split**
* they do not consider the **global optimal tree**

This sometimes creates **unnecessarily complex trees**.

---

## Key Insight

Decision trees **naturally try to reduce impurity to zero**, but doing so often creates **very specific rules** that only work for the training data.
